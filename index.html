Drone-ness is Learnable: Self-Supervised RF Signal Detection with One Centroid and One Threshold

Aryan Thakur
June 2026

Drones are everywhere. Airports, prisons, military bases, they all need to detect RF signals from drone controllers. The standard approach is to collect labeled data for known drone types, train a classifier, and deploy. This works until a new drone controller hits the market and your classifier has no idea what it is. You'd need new labeled data, retraining, redeployment. This doesn't scale.

I wanted to answer a different question: is "drone-ness" a real, learnable signal? Is there something in the RF spectrum that all drone controllers share, that a model could discover on its own, without being told which drone is which? No flight-level labels, no supervised drone-vs-background training. Just raw RF spectrograms and the hypothesis that drone-ness is real.

TL;DR: Yes. IRIS v11 achieves 0.978 AUC on 7 completely unseen drone types using self-supervised learning, with a detection pipeline that's just one centroid and one threshold.

This UMAP tells the whole story. After training on 30 drone types with hierarchical contrastive learning, the embedding space splits into two clean regions: a unified drone cluster (colored) and a background region (grey). The 7 holdout drone types, never seen during training, fall squarely inside the drone cluster. The model didn't memorize training types. It learned the concept of drone-ness.

The Problem

RF spectrograms are messy. Two spectrograms of the same drone controller recorded in different environments can look completely different. Two spectrograms of different devices on the same RF channel can look suspiciously similar. Background noise varies wildly depending on what other transmitters are active, the physical environment, and the SDR hardware.

The real challenge is the matched background problem. The RF channel a drone operates on is shared with WiFi, Bluetooth, and other signals. A naive detector might learn to distinguish "signal present on any channel" from "empty channel," which is trivially easy but completely useless. A meaningful detector must distinguish the drone signal from the channel noise it rides on.

I handle this by synthesizing matched backgrounds: for each drone spectrogram, I create a background with the same RF channel noise but the drone signal surgically removed. The only difference between positive and negative samples is the presence or absence of the drone signal itself. It's the hardest possible test, and it's the one that matters for deployment.

The labeling problem is worth clarifying. I do have type-level labels (I know which drone controller generated each spectrogram, from the data collection protocol). What I don't have is flight-level labels: which individual spectrogram windows contain active drone signals versus pure background. Type labels are free. Flight labels are expensive. My approach uses the free ones and avoids the expensive ones entirely.

The Pipeline

The pipeline is straightforward. An SDR captures the raw RF signal, converted to a 2-channel I/Q spectrogram (256x256). A 6-layer CNN encoder maps this to a 256-dimensional embedding. At detection time, I compute the Mahalanobis distance from any embedding to a single drone centroid and compare against a threshold. Below the threshold, it's a drone.

def detect_drone(embedding, centroid, cov_inv, threshold):
    """Zero-shot drone detection via Mahalanobis distance."""
    diff = embedding - centroid
    mahal = np.sqrt(max(diff @ cov_inv @ diff, 0))
    return mahal < threshold  # closer to centroid = more likely drone

# One centroid from 30 training drone types
centroid = train_embeddings.mean(axis=0)
cov_inv = np.linalg.inv(np.cov(train_embeddings.T) + 1e-3 * np.eye(D))
threshold = 25.55  # Youden's J optimal

One centroid, one threshold. No kNN search, no per-type calibration, no cross-validation scheme. The entire question is whether self-supervised training produces an embedding space where this works.

How the Representation is Learned

The encoder is trained with three objectives working together:

LeJEPA (Latent Embedding Joint-Embedding Predictive Architecture) is the backbone. Instead of reconstructing pixels, it predicts in the latent space: mask out a chunk of the spectrogram, and train a predictor to reconstruct the latent representation of the masked region. This forces the encoder to learn semantic features rather than memorizing noise patterns. The target encoder is an exponential moving average of the online encoder, preventing representation collapse.

SIGReg (Signal-Integrity Guided Regularization) prevents collapse by ensuring variance is spread across all embedding dimensions. It projects embeddings through a fixed random matrix and regularizes each projection's variance toward 1. The weight is tiny (\(\lambda = 0.001\)), just enough to keep the embedding full-rank without distorting learned features.

Hierarchical SupCon is the key innovation, and it deserves its own section.

The total loss combines all three:

\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{total}} = (1 - \lambda) \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{align}} + \lambda \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{SIGReg}} + \alpha \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{HierSupCon}}\]

With \(\lambda = 0.001\), \(\alpha = 0.05\), and temperature \(T = 0.07\). LeJEPA alignment does the heavy lifting. SIGReg is a gentle regularizer. Hierarchical SupCon shapes the geometry. The best checkpoint comes from epoch 5 of 50, consistent with self-supervised learning literature where early checkpoints generalize better.

Hierarchical Supervised Contrastive Learning

This is the main contribution. Standard supervised contrastive loss (SupCon) treats each class as a separate cluster. For 30 drone types plus background, you get 31 isolated islands. Same-type pairs are positives, everything else is negatives. Great for type classification. Wrong for zero-shot detection.

Here's why: a holdout drone type maps to a new cluster far from all 30 training clusters. There's no "drone region," just disconnected islands. The holdout drone floats in the void between clusters, often closer to background than to any drone cluster. In v10, standard SupCon achieved 0.9023 matched AUC but required a complex PCA-kNN-LOTO pipeline that was fragile and hard to interpret.

Hierarchical SupCon operates at two granularity levels simultaneously:

  • Fine-grained loss (weight 0.7): Same-type drone pairs are strong positives, different types plus backgrounds are negatives. The encoder learns that DJI Phantoms look different from FLYSKY controllers, it just doesn't push them apart.
  • Coarse-grained loss (weight 0.3): ALL drone types are weak positives, backgrounds are negatives. This pulls the 30 type clusters into one unified drone region. The encoder learns that despite their differences, all drones share something fundamental: drone-ness.
\[\mathcal{L}_{\text{HierSupCon}} = 0.7 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{fine}} + 0.3 \cdot \mathcal{L}_{\text{coarse}}\] 
def hierarchical_supcon_loss(embeddings, labels, n_drone_types, 
                              temperature=0.07, w_fine=0.7, w_coarse=0.3):
    # Fine-grained: same type = strong positive
    fine_pos = (labels.unsqueeze(0) == labels.unsqueeze(1)) & diag_mask
    
    # Coarse-grained: same superclass (drone vs BG) = weak positive
    super_labels = (labels >= n_drone_types).long()
    coarse_pos = (super_labels.unsqueeze(0) == super_labels.unsqueeze(1)) & diag_mask
    
    L_fine   = supcon_loss_core(embeddings, fine_pos, temperature)
    L_coarse = supcon_loss_core(embeddings, coarse_pos, temperature)
    
    return w_fine * L_fine + w_coarse * L_coarse

The result: drone-ness is the primary axis of variation, type identity is the secondary axis. Holdout types naturally fall within the drone region because the coarse loss taught the encoder that all drones belong together. The fine loss preserves type sub-structure, keeping the encoder learning discriminative features rather than collapsing everything to a single point.

The 0.7/0.3 split isn't arbitrary. Fine-grained needs to dominate because it teaches the encoder to extract meaningful RF features. Without it, the coarse loss would just create a binary classifier that might find trivial shortcuts. More coarse weight erodes per-type discrimination; less fails to unify the clusters.

One Centroid, One Threshold

After training, I compute a single drone centroid by averaging embeddings of all 30 training drone types. For any new embedding, I compute the Mahalanobis distance to this centroid:

\[d_M(z) = \sqrt{(z - \mu_{\text{drone}})^\top \Sigma^{-1} (z - \mu_{\text{drone}})}\]

The threshold is calibrated using Youden's J statistic (maximizing Sensitivity + Specificity - 1). On my validation set: 25.55 with \(J = 0.9978\). The threshold doesn't depend on drone type. It's a single scalar that works for all types, including ones the model has never seen. This is a direct consequence of hierarchical contrastive learning: by merging all drone types into one region, the distance to centroid becomes a type-agnostic measure of drone-ness.

Results: Zero-Shot on 7 Unseen Types

Evaluated on 7 drone types completely held out during training, using matched backgrounds (drone signal removed from same RF channel). This is the hardest test and the one most relevant to deployment.

  • Matched BG AUC: 0.9785
  • Random BG AUC: 1.0000
  • All 7/7 types above 0.95 AUC (minimum: 0.9656 for FUTABA-T14SG)
  • 98.1% of drones closer to centroid than matched backgrounds
  • Bootstrap 95% CI: [0.971, 0.985]

Five types achieve near-perfect detection (AUC >= 0.9984): RadioMaster BOXER (1.0000), WFLY ET10 (1.0000), JUMPER-T14 (0.9999), JR PROPO XG7 (0.9997), and FUTABA-T10J (0.9984). The two weakest, DJI FPV COMBO (0.9674) and FUTABA-T14SG (0.9656), are still well above practical deployment thresholds. The DJI FPV COMBO is a digital HD video transmitter operating in a fundamentally different RF regime than traditional RC controllers, which likely explains why it sits closer to the boundary of the drone region.

Matched BG AUC rises sharply in early epochs then plateaus. Best checkpoint at epoch 5.

The Artifact Test

Are we detecting drones, or just artifacts from the matched background synthesis? Matched backgrounds are created by computationally removing the drone signal, so there could be subtle footprints of that removal process.

Yes, the model can distinguish matched from random backgrounds (AUC 0.9246). That's expected: matched backgrounds share the RF channel with drones, so they have different channel statistics. But the per-pair test is the definitive check: for each holdout drone, compare its distance to the centroid against its matched background's distance. If the model were detecting artifacts, the matched backgrounds would be closer to the centroid than the drones. But 98.1% of drones are closer to the centroid than their matched backgrounds. The model is detecting drone signal, not artifacts.

v9 to v10 to v11

IRIS didn't arrive at v11 on the first try. The progression tells you exactly why hierarchical SupCon matters.

v9 (LeJEPA only): No contrastive loss. The model learns some representation but it's not organized for detection. Matched BG AUC was 0.30, barely above random. The UMAP showed drones and backgrounds completely overlapping. The representation existed somewhere in 256 dimensions but there was no geometric structure for a distance-based detector to exploit.

v10 (+ Standard SupCon): Big jump to 0.9023 matched AUC. But 30 isolated type clusters with no shared drone region. Holdout types fell into the gaps between clusters. One-centroid detection didn't work. I had to use a complex PCA-kNN-LOTO pipeline that was fragile and philosophically unsatisfying. I wanted drone-ness to be a unified concept, not 30 separate ones.

v11 (+ Hierarchical SupCon): The coarse-grained loss changed everything. 0.9785 matched AUC, the 30 clusters merged into a single drone region, and one-centroid detection finally worked. v10 detected drones by checking proximity to any training type. v11 detects drones by checking proximity to drone-ness itself.

Key lesson: Standard SupCon is wrong for zero-shot detection. It optimizes for type discrimination, which is the wrong objective when you need type-agnostic detection. Hierarchical SupCon optimizes for both, producing an embedding space that serves detection without sacrificing feature quality.

Limitations and What's Next

The two weakest holdout types, DJI FPV COMBO (0.9674) and FUTABA-T14SG (0.9656), represent real failure modes. DJI FPV COMBO is a digital HD video link, not a traditional analog RC controller. It operates in a fundamentally different RF regime, and the model struggles to place it firmly inside the drone region. This suggests drone-ness as learned by v11 is still somewhat biased toward traditional RC controller characteristics.

About 1.9% of pairs have the matched background closer to the centroid than the drone. These aren't random: they cluster around specific drone types and tend to occur when the drone signal is weak or atypical. Temporal aggregation (combining decisions across multiple spectrogram windows) would likely eliminate most of these.

Promising directions:

  • More training types: 30 types currently. Adding more, especially digital video links and non-traditional controllers, would expand the drone region and improve edge-case detection.
  • Frequency augmentation: Explicit frequency shifting (simulating different carrier frequencies) might help learn frequency-invariant drone features.
  • Real-world deployment: All evaluations use the RFUAV dataset, recorded in controlled environments. The real test is outdoor over-the-air recording with live drones.

The broader implication: hierarchical contrastive learning is a general strategy for zero-shot transfer to unseen subcategories. Drone detection is one application, but the same principle applies anywhere you need to detect a broad class across many sub-categories. If you can define a label hierarchy and have type-level labels, hierarchical SupCon gives you zero-shot generalization.

Drone-ness is not just a metaphor. It's a latent signal waiting to be discovered, and self-supervised learning with the right training objective can find it.
